Let's see what to use over the counter for this shoulder pain.

To relieve this pain, we will try a non-steroidal anti-inflammatory pain reliever, ice, pain relieving creams or patches and above all resting the shoulder.

With this in mind, see what is recommended below.


NSAIDS click to expand contents

Non-Steroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDS) are useful to reduce the inflammation and pain. These should also help with any stiffness.

Ibuprofen, Naproxen, Aspirin are good examples.

 

Pain relief creams/patches click to expand contents

Pain relief creams and patches are counterirritants or counter stimulants that help mask pain by causing other sensations in the area it was applied to. Sensations such as the feeling of warmth or cold.

They actually cause irritation or inflammation in one location (skin) with the goal of reducing another irritation or inflammation in another location (muscles, joints, tendons).

These creams or patches usually contain  capsaicin, methyl salicylate, camphor, or menthol.    

A good example is Salonpas. This is approved by the FDA for over-the-counter pain relief. Others will do just as good or better. Be sure to compare these patches or creams using the link at the end of this page. This should help you make a good decision on what to get.

 

Worth doing click to expand contents

1. Cold packs help reduce inflammation and swelling. Heat assists in relaxing muscles and increasing circulation.

2. We recommend ice or cold compress within the first 48 to 72 hours of the occurrence of the shoulder pain and heat thereafter. Cold will reduce inflammation that usually occur early on in a sprain.

3. Exercises
