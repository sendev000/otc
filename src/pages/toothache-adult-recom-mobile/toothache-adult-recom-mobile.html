Let's figure out what will give you relief from this toothache.

The goal of this recommendation is to see what you can use over the counter for toothache until you can see a dentist.

With toothache, you will have to see a dentist. The more you delay, the worse it will get.

You should try these in the order listed.


1. Salt water rinse click to expand contents

Place 2 to 3 teaspoonful of table salt in a glass of warm water you can tolerate.

Swiss and spit as a mouth wash for about 30 seconds as needed.

 

2. Pain pills click to expand contents

NonSteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDS)

such as ibuprofen or naproxen would help with the inflammation, pain and swelling.

If you can't take NSAIDs, acetaminophen would be a second choice.

But don't take acetaminophen as a first choice. It is not very good with the inflammatory pain associated with a toothache.

 

3a. Eugenol (clove oil) click to expand contents

Use a Q-tip to apply clove oil to the toothache.​

Place a little folded gauze pad to prevent it from being washed away for maximum effect.

Can't find clove oil?

Look for preparations in the dental aisle containing eugenol. (Red Cross Toothache®, DenTek®).

This is the pain relieving ingredient in clove oil.

If clove oil (eugenol) is not your thing, then go with benzocaine.

 

3b. Benzocaine click to expand contents

Preparations containing 20% benzocaine will help numb the toothache pain for a few hours.

Know that the numbing agent will eventually wash away.

How long depends on the amount of saliva you are producing. So make sure you combine this with something like ibuprofen. 

Average relief time is 4 hours.

Remember, it is clove oil (eugenol) or 20% benzocaine. Not both.

 

4. Cold compress click to expand contents

If swollen and very painful, press an ice pack against the cheek closest to the toothache for about 5 to 10 minutes 3 to 4 times a day to alleviate the pain.

 

Worth mentioning click to expand contents

Massaging the web of the hand between the thumb and index finger on the same side as the body with the toothache pain with ice has been found to produce up to a 50% reduction in acute dental pain [ R. Melzack et ].

I haven't tried it yet, but it would be interesting to see the result the next time I have a toothache.

If you tried it, please post your experience in the comments section.
