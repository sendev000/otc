How long?
You would consider yourself constipated if you have most of the symptoms listed below.

 

  • Have a bowel movement of less than 3 times a week.

  • Have hard stools.

  • Have difficulty passing stools.

  • Have pain while passing stools.

  • Unable to pass stool after pushing for more than 10 minutes.

  • Have a sense of incomplete evacuation after stooling.

With this constipation, are you experiencing nausea and/or vomiting?

Feeling bloated?

 

Seek urgent care or call the doctor.

Here is why:

  • This may suggest serious obstruction.

Require examination and tests by the doctor such as:

  • Flat and upright abdominal x-rays
  • Water-soluble contrast enema to evaluate for colonic obstruction.
  • Barium x-ray of the small intestine
  • Possibly a CT scan
  • Sigmoidoscopy or
  • Colonoscopy.

Is this constipation alternating with watery diarrhea?

 

Seek urgent care or call your doctor.

Here is why:

  • You may be looking at fecal impaction here. This is serious.
  • May also be looking at Irritable bowel syndrome

Call now if the diarrhea contains:

  • Watery mucus
  • particles of feces
  • and you have stomach cramps.

Do you delay going to the bathroom when you have the urge to go?

 

Yes?
You will have to stop this. This is a major cause of constipation.

This is one reason travellers always have constipation. Aside from having to sit in one place for a long time leading to lack of physical activity.

 

What about your diet? Is it low in fiber?

Are you currently taking any of the groups of medications listed below?

Medications that are:

  • Antihistamines
  • Antispasmodics
  • Antiparkinsonian drugs
  • Ca channel blockers
  • Opioid pain relievers
  • Antipsychotics
  • Iron, aluminum, Calcium, Bismuth?

 

Yes?
These can cause constipation.

If you just started taking the medications, discuss this with your doctor.

If you are taking iron suplements, switch to the ones that will cause less constipation. The pharmacist may discuss this with you later.

Note that:

  1. Stool that is black may mean that the blood is coming from the upper part of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract (esophagus, stomach, and the first part of the small intestine).
  2. Bright red blood or maroon-colored stools may mean that the blood is coming from the lower part of the GI tract (large bowel, rectum, or anus).
